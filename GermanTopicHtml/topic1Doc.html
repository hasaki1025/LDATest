In the last decade a decidedly computational turn in scholarly editing is noticeable. Texts are not just encoded anymore. To store and execute scholarly decisions increasingly also program code is applied, which thereby becomes a computational expression of scholarly theory. Theorists and practitioners of such computational approaches have not just put forward scalability and replicability as motivations for computational methods, but also the promise of increased analytical power and on the horizon a changed epistemology. Reflectance Transformation Imaging (RTI) has many uses in imaging heritage items such as coins, seals, and small archaeological artefacts. Hand (highlight) RTI is time-consuming, and domes are the preferred solution. Unfortunately, commercial domes cost in the range of €10.000–30.000, out of reach for teaching purposes. The SimpleRTIDome project is putting together a functional dome which will require no soldering, combining 3D printed parts with readily available single-board computer (SBC) boards, designed to be assembled by individuals with no particular "Maker" skills. The target cost in materials for the final dome, including built-in camera, is €200. I have built a working prototype at this price point which will be refined between now and the conference. A full parts list and (simple) instructions will be released via GitHub and the Uni Graz. The same basic controller setup supports a larger and more capable research dome, targeted in the €600–800 range.
